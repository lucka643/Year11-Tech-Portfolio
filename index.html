    Brief

    Problem  ·  Situation  ·  Target  ·  Brief

    1a · Design Problem

    Plastic that never breaks down.

    Most of the toys on the shelves are made from plastic that landfills can't break down. They're cheap to make and buy, and companies aren't looking to use other materials because biodegradable and recyclable options cost more.

    Using eco-friendly materials would let toys either break down naturally or be reused, cutting out a huge amount of the plastic waste being produced today.

    1b · Situation

    Built to survive rough play.

    The audience for this product is children who are hard on their toys. It has to stand up to being thrown, dropped and stepped on without falling apart.

    Just as importantly, it can't pose any safety risk — no sharp edges and no small removable parts that could be swallowed.

    1c · Target Market

    Loved by kids, bought by parents.

    Although children are the main users, they usually don't have the money to buy the product themselves. To sell it, the design first has to grab the child's attention and interest — which means age-appropriate toys they'll genuinely enjoy and connect with.

    It also has to appeal to parents, caregivers, family and friends, since they're the ones who make the final purchase decision.

    1e · Design Brief

    The brief.

    Supporting the development of young children requires a thoughtful balance of modern and traditional ideas. The design should consider the different environments where children learn and interact — classrooms, social settings, independent activities and group play. While children may influence buying decisions, parents and families ultimately purchase products, so the design must also appeal to them by showing clear educational value.

    The product should be based on the client's needs and preferences, with market research like surveys and group discussions used to gather feedback. The first focus is an attractive appearance, followed by practical features that let it function effectively. Cost affects manufacturing methods and material choices — for this prototype, timber, plastic and metal are considered, with attention to local availability and environmental impact. Anthropometrics and ergonomics are also key, to keep the product comfortable, safe and suitable for children to use.

    Research

    Plan  ·  Product Analysis  ·  Aesthetics  ·  Materials

    2a · Research Plan

    What I need to find out.

    Reliability

    How easily the toy can be repaired decides how long it survives — especially for young children who are tough on their things. A toy that gets fixed instead of binned is central to my eco-friendly brief.

    Information Needed
    • Ways the toy can be fixed if a part breaks
    • Which joints are most likely to fail
    • What makes a repair simple for a parent at home
    Resources / Approach
    • Methods to strengthen wooden joints (dowels, glue types)
    • Possibly include a small tube of glue or spare parts
    • Design parts to be individually replaceable

    Why it matters: a long-lasting, repairable toy keeps plastic and timber out of landfill — the core problem this whole project is solving.

    Materials

    The right material has to balance durability, safety, cost and environmental impact all at once. This research feeds directly into the Materials section later in the portfolio.

    Information Needed
    • The best wood for the shape and strength needed
    • A suitable non-toxic paint or finish
    Resources / Approach
    • Compare local woods: pine, beech, Fijian mahogany
    • Strong woods (oak, maple, cherry) vs decorative (cedar, pine, poplar)
    • Research child-safe paints and varnishes

    Why it matters: the material choice affects safety, cost, weight and how eco-friendly the finished toy actually is.

    Aesthetics

    A toy first has to catch a child's eye before any of its other qualities matter. Colour, shape and finish all decide whether it gets picked up off the shelf in the first place.

    Information Needed
    • Colour combinations that work well together
    • Styles and themes children are drawn to
    • How appearance can improve how the toy is used
    Resources / Approach
    • Review successful toys already on the market
    • Study colour theory — complementary colours, bold primaries vs pastels
    • Analyse the look of popular wooden toys

    Why it matters: children influence the buying decision, so visual appeal is what gets a parent to the checkout.

    Playability

    Playability is why a child keeps coming back to a toy. It depends on the size, how it's handled, and how engaging the interaction is.

    Information Needed
    • A comfortable size for small hands
    • How the toy will actually be played with
    • Ways to make it more engaging (moving parts, sound)
    Resources / Approach
    • Research average children's hand sizes (anthropometrics)
    • Look at popular toys and current trends
    • Test grip and part sizes on real users

    Why it matters: the moving wings, ailerons and opening canopy on my jet are all aimed squarely at playability.

    Manufacturing

    I also need to confirm the toy can actually be built with the tools and skills available in the workshop — efficiently, safely, and to a good standard.

    Information Needed
    • Which machines and tools each part needs
    • How each part is cut, shaped and joined
    Resources / Approach
    • Learn the workshop machinery — bandsaw, disc sander, laser cutter, drill press
    • Plan the most efficient order to build parts
    • Keep every process within my skill level

    Why it matters: a great design is useless if I can't actually make it — manufacturing keeps the project realistic.

    2b · Product Analysis #1

    Hot Wheels vs Buzzy Bee.

    Hot Wheels

    Mattel · ≈ $2.80 NZD
    Repairability
    Not repairable. If the wheels or axles break, it's cheaper to replace the whole toy than fix it.
    Material
    Die-cast metal body with plastic parts.
    Aesthetics
    Small, detailed and eye-catching. Realistic styling and bright colours appeal to children and collectors.
    Playability
    Rolls smoothly, easy to use with tracks or floor play. Good for quick, imaginative play and collecting.
    Manufacturing
    Mass-produced, keeping the price low and availability high.
    Environment
    Metal can be recycled, but plastic parts reduce sustainability. Small size uses fewer materials.
    Ergonomics
    Small and lightweight, fits comfortably in one hand.

    Buzzy Bee Pull Toy

    Buzzy Bee · ≈ $39.95 NZD
    Repairability
    More repairable than plastic toys because it's wood. Small damaged parts can often be glued or replaced.
    Material
    Wood with a non-toxic finish.
    Aesthetics
    Bright, classic and recognisable. Its colourful design gives it a strong New Zealand identity.
    Playability
    Easy for toddlers to pull along. The clicking sound and moving wings make it interactive and fun.
    Manufacturing
    Made as a traditional wooden toy — a more classic, durable feel than modern plastic toys.
    Environment
    Wooden body is renewable and longer-lasting than plastic. Non-toxic paint is a plus.
    Ergonomics
    Easy pull-string handle and lightweight size for toddlers.

    2c · Aesthetics Analysis

    Colour & feel.

    Colour

    Bright primary colours like red, blue and yellow attract attention and create excitement. Colours opposite each other on the colour wheel complement each other and help products stand out, while softer pastels create a calm, friendly look for younger children.

    Feel

    Smooth surfaces feel comfortable, safe and pleasant to touch, giving a higher-quality feel that suits children's products. Rough surfaces feel unfinished and less appealing — sharp or uneven textures also reduce safety and comfort.

    Examples

    Rubik's Cube — bright contrasting colours that are easy to recognise; smooth plastic and rounded edges feel good to hold and the turning motion is satisfying.

    NeeDoh — vibrant, playful colours; soft, stretchy, squishy texture that's relaxing to squeeze, with a smooth surface that encourages repeated sensory use.

    2d · Materials

    Wood options.

    Pine

    Pine

    Pine is a softwood and one of the cheapest, most available timbers in New Zealand. It's pale, lightweight and very easy to cut, sand and glue — which makes it perfect for fast prototyping and testing shapes before committing to a more expensive wood.

    Pros
    • Lightweight and easy to handle
    • Soft enough to cut and shape by hand or machine
    • Inexpensive (~NZ$10.95 per linear metre)
    • Readily available everywhere
    • Takes paint, glue and varnish well
    • Fast-growing and renewable
    Cons
    • Soft surface dents and scratches easily
    • Can warp, split or crack over time
    • Knots create weak points in thin parts
    • Least durable of my three options

    For this project: pine is ideal for early models and test pieces, but its softness is a real risk on thin parts like the jet's wings and tail — a child throwing or stepping on it could snap or dent those areas. Better as a prototyping wood than the final toy.

    Fijian Mahogany

    Fijian Mahogany

    Fijian mahogany is a plantation-grown hardwood with a rich reddish-brown colour and a tight, even grain. It's a premium, furniture-grade timber that's prized for its strength, durability and clean finish — and importantly it's grown sustainably.

    Pros
    • Strong and very durable
    • Beautiful reddish grain & premium finish
    • Moisture and rot resistant
    • Splinter resistant — safer for children
    • Plantation-grown, so eco-friendly
    • Machines and sands cleanly
    Cons
    • More expensive than pine
    • Heavier to work with and carry
    • Harder to source locally
    • Arguably overkill for a small model

    For this project: the durability and splinter resistance make this a strong candidate for the final toy, where it needs to survive years of rough play safely. Its sustainable sourcing also lines up directly with the eco-friendly design brief.

    Beech

    Beech

    Beech is a pale, fine-grained hardwood that has been the classic choice for wooden toys for generations — think building blocks, trains and pull-along toys. It's strong, machines beautifully and finishes to a very smooth, splinter-free surface.

    Pros
    • Strong and hard-wearing
    • Very fine, even grain → smooth, safe finish
    • Machines, turns and sands cleanly
    • Good value for a hardwood (~$11.50 / 8"×24")
    • Traditional, proven toy material
    Cons
    • Heavy compared to pine
    • Absorbs moisture / can warp if unsealed
    • Not rot-resistant outdoors

    For this project: beech is the strongest all-round option — tough enough to handle being thrown and stepped on, yet fine-grained enough to sand splinter-free for a child-safe finish. Its long history in toy-making makes it a safe, sensible choice for the build.

    2d · Materials

    Plastic & metal options.

    Acrylic

    Acrylic

    Acrylic (PMMA) is a clear or coloured plastic that can be laser-cut and shaped very precisely. Its glassy, transparent finish makes it the obvious candidate for a part like a see-through cockpit canopy on the jet.

    Pros
    • Lightweight yet strong
    • Laser-cuts and shapes precisely
    • Weather and water resistant
    • Smooth, glossy finish
    • Available clear or in bright colours
    Cons
    • Scratches easily
    • Can crack or shatter under sharp impact
    • Not biodegradable or recyclable easily
    • Cut edges can be sharp
    • Can be expensive

    For this project: perfect for a transparent canopy, but it pulls against the eco-friendly brief — so I'd limit it to a single small feature part rather than the body, and sand any cut edges smooth for child safety.

    Aluminium

    Aluminium

    Aluminium is a lightweight silver-grey metal that's strong for its weight, corrosion resistant and fully recyclable. It's used anywhere a clean, modern, low-maintenance metal part is needed.

    Pros
    • Lightweight for a metal
    • Rust and corrosion resistant
    • Strong relative to its weight
    • Fully recyclable
    • Clean, modern appearance
    Cons
    • More expensive than steel
    • Dents under impact
    • Needs metalworking tools to shape
    • Cold and hard — less inviting for a toy

    For this project: could suit a small structural fitting or an axle, but metal parts add cost and take away from the warm, safe, wooden feel I'm aiming for. Best kept to hidden fittings if used at all.

    Steel

    Steel

    Steel is a very strong, widely available metal alloy used wherever maximum strength and durability matter. It's the toughest material I looked at — but also the heaviest and least suited to a young child's toy.

    Pros
    • Extremely strong and durable
    • Very long lasting
    • Cost-effective
    • Widely available
    Cons
    • Heavy
    • Rusts if left untreated
    • Hard to cut and shape
    • Hard, cold and unsafe edges for young kids

    For this project: too heavy and hard for a children's toy, and shaping it needs equipment beyond what's practical. More useful for the jigs and tooling used to make the toy than for the toy itself.
